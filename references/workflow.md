# Workflow

## Goal

Turn a narrative into a reusable research object with explicit chain position, timing, and falsification.

## Steps

### 1. Anchor the theme

Write the theme in one sentence:

- What is expanding?
- Why now?
- What real system has to change?

Good examples:

- "CPO adoption forces more precision optical alignment and packaging throughput."
- "HBM expansion shifts the bottleneck from memory demand to packaging and test throughput."
- "AI data-center density makes power conversion and liquid cooling more binding."

### 2. Find the first hard constraint

Ask:

- What fails first if demand doubles?
- Which layer cannot be added quickly?
- Which layer requires customer qualification or scarce materials?

Prioritize constraints that are physical, procedural, or capacity-based.

### 3. Split the chain into layers

Common buckets:

- end demand;
- system vendors;
- module and subsystem vendors;
- chips and devices;
- packaging and test;
- equipment;
- materials and consumables;
- infrastructure and deployment.

Then split again until the economics become specific enough to matter.

Example:

- CPO is too broad.
- Better: CW laser, FAU, PMLA, optical foundry, thermal cage, SiPh substrate, optical test.

### 4. Judge scarcity

Use these questions:

- How many credible suppliers exist?
- How long is qualification?
- Can customers dual-source easily?
- Is scale-up gated by tooling, purity, know-how, or yield?
- Is capacity already being reserved?

Rank layers first. Do not jump to tickers too early.

### 5. Map public companies

For each company, classify it as:

- direct bottleneck controller;
- close supplier to the bottleneck;
- indirect beneficiary;
- theme stock with weak mapping.

Strong mapping requires more than thematic similarity.

### 6. Add timing

Use stage labels:

- concept;
- sample;
- qualification;
- pilot;
- low-volume;
- ramp;
- mass production;
- expansion.

Then assign a window:

- this quarter;
- next 6 months;
- next 12 months;
- 2-3 years;
- too early.

### 7. Add re-rating triggers

Typical triggers:

- customer confirmation;
- design win;
- qualification completion;
- capex or expansion plan;
- order disclosure;
- margin inflection;
- new analyst coverage;
- acquisition;
- policy or defense procurement inclusion.

### 8. Add kill conditions

Always include at least one concrete downgrade path:

- weak mapping;
- substitution risk;
- revenue exposure too small;
- timing too far out;
- valuation already prices success;
- debt, dilution, or governance burden;
- main legacy business overwhelms the new story.

## Ranking guidance

Separate these scores conceptually:

1. Chain truth
2. Mapping purity
3. Timing quality
4. Market mispricing

High chain truth but weak mapping goes to observation, not conviction.
